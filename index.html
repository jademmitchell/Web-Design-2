Giant Pandas 101

Lets protect the Giant Pandas! This website aims to help raise awarness for these beautiful creatures. Despite their change in status from 'endangered species' to 'vulnerable species', and their relative lack of natural predators, pandas are still at risk. Severe threats from humans have left just over 1,800 pandas in the wild.

The Giant Panda

Scientific Name: Ailuropoda melanoleuca
Habitat: Temperate broadleaf and mixed forests of Southwest China
Status: Vulnerable
Population: 1864 in the wild

Diet

Giant pandas spend 10-16 hours a day feeding, mainly on bamboo.
Pandas are 99% vegetarian, although
their digestive system is typical of a carnivore.

Height and Weight

Height: adults can grow more than 1.2m.
Weight: Females can grow to 100kg, while Males can grow to 150kg.
A newborn panda is about 1/900th
the size of its mother.

Habitat

Their black and white fur helps them camouflage in their environment.
White: blend into snowy backgrounds.
Black: blend into shade and forest.
They are excellent climbers despite
their bulk.

FACT #1

Giant pandas first became endangered in 1990 due to excessive poaching in the 80s and deforestation, depleting their bamboo food source. Now, Giant Pandas main threat is loss of bamboo.

FACT #2

In 2016, the Giant Panda officially moved from the IUCN’s Global Red List of species at risk of extinction from “endangered” to “vulnerable”, following a population growth of nearly 17% over the past decade.

FACT #3

Although poaching impacted pandas in the past, its impact declined since the enactment of the Wildlife Protection Act (1988), which bans poaching. However, pandas may get caught accidentally in snares set for other species.

FACT #4

For the 1% of their diet that isn't bamboo, pandas eat eggs, small animals, carrion, and forage in farmland for pumpkin, kidney beans, wheat and domestic pig food.

FACT #5

Infrastructure development (such as dams, roads, and railways) is increasingly fragmenting and isolating panda populations, preventing pandas from finding new bamboo forests and potential mates.

FACT #6

Mother pandas keep contact with their cub nearly 100% of the time during their first month. At 5 months old, giant pandas learn how to climb - sometimes practicing by climbing on their mum.

How we've helped Giant Pandas

WWF (The World Wildlife Fund) is an international conservation organization. They help with conservation decisions and approaches, and equip people with the tools and knowledge they need to protect pandas and their habitat. They work towards and advocate for...

  • increasing the area of panda habitat under legal protection,
  • building local capacities for nature reserve management,
  • patrolling against poaching and illegal logging, and
  • continuing research and monitoring.

Thanks to WWF's program, panda reserves now cover more than 3.8 million acres of forest.
